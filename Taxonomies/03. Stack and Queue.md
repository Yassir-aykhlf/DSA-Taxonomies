# Stack & Queue

> A stack gives last-in-first-out access and a queue gives first-in-first-out access, turning order-of-processing into the core of backtracking, monotonic scans, and breadth-first exploration.

> **Use when**
> - You match nested structure — brackets, tags, expressions — where the most recent open must close first (stack).
> - You need the next greater/smaller element or histogram spans: a monotonic stack answers each in amortized O(1).
> - You explore level by level or find shortest unweighted paths (queue-driven BFS, including multi-source).
> - You maintain a window extreme as it slides (monotonic deque) or need both-ended O(1) access.
> - You design a structure with ordered guarantees: min-stack, circular queue, LRU-style history.

> **Complexity**
> - Stack/queue push, pop, peek: **O(1)**; a monotonic-stack sweep over n items: **O(n)** total.
> - BFS over a graph: **O(V + E)** time, **O(V)** space for the queue and visited set.
> - Monotonic-deque sliding window over n elements: **O(n)** time, **O(k)** space.

## Related patterns

- **DFS** — a stack (explicit or the call stack) drives depth-first exploration.
- **BFS** — a queue powers level-by-level expansion and shortest unweighted paths.
- **Heap & Priority Queue** — a priority queue is the ordered specialization of a queue.
- **Sliding Window** — a monotonic deque maintains the window's extreme in O(1) amortized.
