# Divide and Conquer

> Break a problem into independent subproblems, solve each recursively, then combine their results into the answer for the whole.

> **Use when**
> - A problem splits cleanly into similar, independent halves whose solutions merge cheaply (merge/quick sort, closest pair).
> - You want a count or aggregate computed during a merge step (inversions, smaller-after-self, range sums).
> - Search or selection over a halvable space (binary search, quickselect, k-th element).
> - A value is defined recursively over a structure — trees built from traversals, interval splits, parenthesizations.
> - Fast math by halving the exponent or operand size (binary exponentiation, Karatsuba, matrix power).

> **Complexity**
> - Balanced split with linear merge: **O(n log n)** (merge sort, count-of-smaller).
> - Halve-and-discard: **O(log n)** (binary search, fast exponentiation).
> - Analyzed via the Master Theorem on `T(n) = a·T(n/b) + f(n)`.

## Related patterns

- **Dynamic Programming** — D&C with overlapping subproblems that are cached instead of recomputed.
- **Binary Search** — the simplest D&C: discard half the space each step.
- **Greedy Algorithms** — sometimes a cheaper alternative when a local choice suffices.
- **Recursion** — the control structure every divide-and-conquer algorithm is built on.
